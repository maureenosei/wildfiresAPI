Blue Jay Fire

The Blue Jay Fire was ignited by lightning July 24. The fire started approximately 4 miles south of White Wolf campground and is located primarily in Wilderness on the south side of Tioga Road at 9,000 feet in elevation. Lightning-caused wildfires are a natural part of the Yosemite National Park Wilderness. The size of the fire is fairly small as it burned 6,922 acres and is 90 percent contained. This fire is one of many that has occurred in the Yosemite National Park area.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 6,922 acres

San Dimas Fire

The San Dimas brush fire burned through 132 acres of land on Friday, November 6th 2020 -- injuring 2 firefighters in the process. Although extremely dangerous, it burned away from homes and did not force any mandatory evacuations, however, during the height of the fire ash fell on surrounding neighborhoods. In addition, the San Dimas golf course served as a buffer between the fire and surrounding homes. The administrative unit for this fire was the Angeles National Forest and Los Angeles County Fire Department. The cause of this fire remains unknown.

  • Casualties: 3 injuries
  • Area Burned: 132,000 acres

Coleman and Dolan Fires

Because of their proximity to one another -- both geographically and within a certain time frame, the Coleman and Dolan fires are usually regarded as one. The Dolan Fire began on August 18, 2020, and grew to approximately 125,000 acres. The cause of this fire is still under investigation. In regards to the Coleman Fire, it began on October 18th, soon after the Dolan fire began to die down. The Dolan fire burned through only 574 acres, however, it is still an active fire. The cause of the Dolan fire is still under investigation as well. In total, over 230 personnel were assigned to help fight the fires although no mandatory evacuations were in place. Nineteen structures were destroyed by these two fires.

  • Casualties: No Casualties
  • Area Burned: 125,000 acres

Get more information at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/

Wolf Fire

Unlike other fires, the start of the Wolf Fire has been discovered to be of natural causes - lightning. The fire began on August 11, 2020, and is still an active fire. However, winter conditions and precipitation is expected to reduce current fire activity. The Wolf fire is pretty small, as it covers only 2,057 acres. However, multiple public safety orders have been called by authorities. There are no casualties.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 2,057 acres

Bulldog Fire

This fire is not related to the Creek Fire, it is a separate start discovered on September 09, 2020 at approximately 3:09 PM. It is being managed by the Sierra National Forest using a confine and contain strategy taking advantage of trails, natural features, and hand crews to fully suppress this fire. The Bullfrog Fire has burned 1185 acres and is 60% contained. Crews remain on scene to continue to monitor the suppression lines on the east side of the fire and to strengthen and improve control lines as needed. The west side of the fire will be allowed to burn until it reaches natural barriers keeping the fire contained. There is no threat to life or property.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 1,185 acres

Get more information at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/

Slater Fire

The Slater Fire started on September 8, near Slater Butte Fire Lookout on the Klamath National Forest. It is currently burning on the Klamath, Six Rivers, and Rogue-Siskiyou National Forests in Siskiyou and Del Norte Counties in California and Josephine County in Oregon. The fire is fairly large -- having burned through 157,229 acres with only an 87% containment. Road closure and evacuations are in place due to the Slater Fire. The cause of this fire is still under investigation.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 157,229 acres

Get more information at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/

Bobcat Fire

The Bobcat Fire in Los Angeles County has scorched more than 100,000 acres as of Monday, making it one of the largest fires in the county's history. It is one of at least 27 major wildfires currently burning in California, where 26 people have died and 6,400 structures have been destroyed since fire activity picked up in August, the California Department of Fire Protection (Cal Fire) said. The cause of the Bobcat Fire, which started on September 6, is still unknown. More than 1,700 fire personnel are battling the blaze. Cal Fire urged residents to stay vigilant as "the most devastating wildfires" typically happen in September and October.

  • Casualties: 26 deaths
  • Area Burned: 115,796 acres

El Dorado Fire

A fire sparked by a pyrotechnic device used during a gender reveal party has burned more than 10,000 acres since Saturday. The El Dorado Fire in Southern California's San Bernardino County has scorched 10,574 acres and was 16% contained as of Tuesday, according to fire officials, who say it's "one of the most dangerous fires" they've seen in the area.The fire was caused by a "smoke-generating pyrotechnic device" used at a party on Saturday morning in El Dorado Ranch Park in Yucaipa, about 70 miles east of Los Angeles, Cal Fire investigators determined.

  • Casualties: 13 injured
  • Area Burned: 22,744 acres

Creek Fire

The Creek Fire was first sparked on Friday, September 4, and 379,895 acres have burned as of Tuesday morning with 78% containment. CAL FIRE officials say it is the largest single fire in California's recorded history. At least 927 structures have been damaged or destroyed, and 105 are threatened. The wildfire caused 30,000 residents of Fresno County and 15,000 residents of Madera County to evacuate when it first broke out. Firefighters continue to have success with suppression efforts on the northwestern side of the wildfire. On Tuesday, officials closed the fire camp in Oakhurst, and Central Camp Road reopened to permit holders. It is still closed to the public.

  • Casualties: 1 death
  • Area Burned: 379,895 acres

Get more information at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/

Rattlesnake Fire

The Rattlesnake Fire was discovered on August 16 after substantial lightning occurred over the area. It is located within the Sequoia and Kings Canyon Wilderness in the Kern River drainage. The fire is burning in steep inaccessible terrain with sparse ground fuels and pockets of dead and down trees. While the size of the fire has increased, there are no current threats to life or property. The park is using a confine and contain full-suppression strategy utilizing natural rock features, existing trails, and other natural barriers as containment lines. Not only does this limit exposure to firefighters and minimizes impacts in the wilderness, it allows firefighting resources to be available to battle other widfires across the area and state that have a greater threat to life and property.

  • Casualties: 26 deaths
  • Area Burned: 115,796 acres

Slink Fire

The Slink Fire was reported on August 29th at 6 p.m. in the Slinkard Valley southwest of Topaz Lake as a result of lightning in the area. The Slink Fire is 90% contained and it has spanned 26,759 acres. Because of steep and rugged terrain and the priority on firefighter safety, the last 10% of the fire located in the Carson Iceberg Wilderness will not have fireline constructed around it and will continue to smolder until it receives precipitation.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 26,759 acres

Get more information at https://inciweb.nwcg.gov/

Apple Fire

The Apple fire, which is currently inactive, has destroyed structures and caused 4 inquiries to both fire personnel and citizens. The cause of the fire was burning carbon emitted from the tailpipe of a vehicle. Twelve structures and two outbuildings were destroyed. The Apple and burned through 33,424 acres. The fire ignited on July 21, 2020, as three separate blazes. Significant mandatory evacuations for numerous communities were ordered.

  • Casualties: No casualties
  • Area Burned: 33,424 acres
